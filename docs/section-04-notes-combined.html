Main Components of Constructivism in the Classroom

  1. Elicit prior knowledge: Begin lessons by activating students' existing knowledge through pre-tests, interviews, or group activities that require recalling prior knowledge.
  2. Create cognitive dissonance: Challenge students with problems and activities that push them to encounter new situations, prompting them to revise their existing understanding and build new knowledge.
  3. Apply knowledge with feedback: Encourage students to evaluate new information and modify their existing knowledge. Provide opportunities for comparing pre-existing schemas to novel situations through presentations, discussions, and quizzes.
  4. Reflect on learning: Allow students to demonstrate their learning through activities such as presentations, reflexive papers, or creating tutorials for their peers, giving them a chance to showcase their understanding.

These components emphasize the importance of activating prior knowledge, promoting active engagement with challenging problems, providing feedback for learning application, and fostering reflection on acquired knowledge.

Constructivist learning theory is based on the idea that learners construct their own understanding and knowledge of the world through experiencing things and reflecting on those experiences. It emphasizes the active role of the learner in building understanding and making sense of information.

Authentic assessment, on the other hand, is a form of assessment in which students are asked to perform real-world tasks that demonstrate meaningful application of essential knowledge and skills. It mirrors the tasks and problem-solving that are encountered in the world outside of school.

These two concepts align in several ways:

  1. Active Learning: Both authentic assessment and constructivist learning theory emphasize the active role of the learner. In constructivist learning, students are actively constructing their own knowledge. Similarly, in authentic assessment, students are actively involved in the assessment process through real-world tasks.

  2. Contextual Learning: Constructivist theory emphasizes that learning is deeply contextual and is influenced by the environment in which it occurs. Authentic assessment reinforces this by requiring students to apply their knowledge and skills in real-world contexts.

  3. Complex Problem Solving: Constructivism posits that meaningful learning occurs when learners grapple with complex, real-world problems. Authentic assessment aligns with this by presenting students with complex tasks that mirror those they will encounter in the world beyond the classroom.

  4. Reflection and Metacognition: Both constructivist learning and authentic assessment value reflection and metacognition. Constructivist learning theory sees reflection as a key part of the learning process, where learners reflect on and interpret their experiences to construct new understanding. Authentic assessment often incorporates reflection as well, asking students to think about their work, their learning processes, and the implications of their findings.

  5. Collaboration: Both constructivist learning theory and authentic assessment often emphasize the value of collaboration. Constructivism recognizes that our understandings are socially constructed, and authentic assessments often involve collaborative tasks.

In essence, authentic assessment can be seen as an extension of constructivist learning theory into the realm of assessment. It carries forward the principles of active, contextual, problem-based, reflective, and collaborative learning into the way students' understanding and skills are evaluated.
