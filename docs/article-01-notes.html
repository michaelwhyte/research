Defintion

EdD: EdD stands for Doctor of Education. It is a doctoral degree that is focused on professional practice and leadership in the field of education. The EdD program is designed for individuals who are interested in pursuing advanced studies and research in education, with an emphasis on practical application and addressing real-world problems in educational settings. The EdD degree is often pursued by educators, administrators, and other professionals working in educational institutions who seek to enhance their knowledge, skills, and leadership abilities in order to make meaningful contributions to the field of education. (ChatGPT 3.5)

PoP: In the context of education, a Problem of Practice (PoP) refers to a persistent, specific issue or challenge that is embedded in the work of a professional practitioner within an educational setting. It is a problem that practitioners encounter in their day-to-day work, which has the potential to be addressed and solved through research, analysis, and practical application.

A PoP is typically a complex and contextualized problem that requires deep understanding of the educational system, its stakeholders, and the broader social and cultural factors influencing it. It goes beyond theoretical or abstract concerns and focuses on real-world issues that practitioners face in their professional roles.

Identifying and studying a PoP is a key component of EdD programs, as it allows doctoral students to delve into practical, field-based research and develop solutions that can positively impact educational practice. By investigating PoPs, EdD students aim to gain insights, propose innovative strategies, and contribute to improving educational outcomes, experiences, and understanding within their specific contexts. Findings

Research Question #1: How Do EdD Students Derive their Problems of Practice (PoPs)?

Here are the key points summarized as bullet points:

  • The 28 analyzed dissertations revealed that all the problems of practice (PoPs) were derived from either a "felt difficulty" or a "real-world dilemma" experienced by the doctoral students in their local contexts.
  • "Felt difficulties" referred to deep concerns or dissatisfactions that practitioners felt a strong urge to address through direct action in their dissertation research.
  • Examples of "felt difficulties" included addressing the needs of English Language Learners in writing instruction, reevaluating Tier Three instructional support in special education, and addressing the academic performance gap between music and other subjects for female students.
  • On the other hand, "real-world dilemmas" represented situations where practitioners had to navigate existing tensions or challenges within larger educational policies and procedures.
  • Examples of "real-world dilemmas" included dealing with frustrations of teachers implementing new state standards and balancing support for struggling and advanced learners in a detracked honors geometry classroom.
  • The distinction between "felt difficulties" and "real-world dilemmas" was valuable in helping EdD students identify and frame their PoPs, and both types were found across various professional roles and contexts.
  • A table provided an overview of the dissertations, their titles, the authors' professional roles, and how the PoPs were categorized.
  • Among the analyzed dissertations, 8 fell into the category of "felt difficulties" while 20 fell into the category of "real-world dilemmas," suggesting the prevalence and complexity of dilemmas faced by EdD students.
  • Regardless of the specific categorization, the significance lies in recognizing the value of both "felt difficulties" and "real-world dilemmas" in helping EdD students identify and frame their PoPs.

Research Question #2: What Is the Nature of These Problems of Practice (PoPs) that Our Students Have Studied?

Here are the key points summarized as bullet points:

  • The 28 analyzed dissertations fell into three main categories of Problems of Practice (PoPs): supporting marginalized students or groups (14 dissertations), increasing the quality of educator professional development (9 dissertations), and supporting novices' entry into the profession (3 dissertations).
  • Within the category of supporting marginalized students or groups, examples included African-American male and female students, English Language Learners, disengaged Haitian American students, struggling and advanced learners, students in early childhood education, visually impaired students, gifted students, LGBTQ students, and students at hard-to-staff schools.
  • Nine dissertations focused on improving the quality of educator professional development, addressing the need for effective professional learning opportunities, introducing frameworks and reflective tools, and challenging deficit views about specific groups of learners.
  • Three dissertations focused on supporting novices' entry into the profession, including supporting new teachers and new principals, aiming to enhance their effectiveness and address challenges they face.
  • The dissertations reflected a passion for making a difference in marginalized students' lives, aligned with the principle of addressing equity, ethics, and social justice in EdD programs.
  • The findings highlighted the significance of supporting marginalized students, enhancing professional development, and assisting novices in the education profession as areas of focus in EdD dissertations.

Research Question #3: What Are the Reported Impacts the Study of Problems of Practice (PoPs) Have on Doctoral Students’ Local Contexts?

Here are the key points summarized as bullet points:

  • Five generic themes of impact emerged from the analyzed dissertations:

    1. Advancing/changing researchers' practice and beliefs
    2. Promoting communication and collaboration
    3. Strengthening/informing policy
    4. Supporting student learning
    5. Fostering colleagues' learning

  • Coding and discussing findings related to the impact of the study of Problems of Practice (PoPs) was challenging due to dissertations having multiple impacts simultaneously and the specific and contextual nature of the impact described by each student.

  • Decontextualizing impact from the specific dissertation and local context would diminish its meaning and significance.

  • There was a wide variety in the ways students discussed and reported impact, leading to different interpretations by the researchers.

  • To gain a more accurate understanding of the impact of dissertation studies, the researchers are embarking on a new study using survey and interview data collection strategies to assess practitioner scholarship after students graduate from the EdD program.

  • The additional data sources are expected to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the impact, and further details about the derived impact categories will be described in the future study.

Conclusion

...four principles for deriving PoPs to assist doctoral students across the nation in framing EdD dissertation studies. These principles include:

  1. PoPs are deeply embedded in the students’ professional practice or context.

  2. PoPs emanate from felt difficulties and real-world dilemmas students face as they work as educational practitioners.

  3. PoPs align with contemporary, critical issues in education explicated in the literature, such as creating more equitable schooling experiences for all children.

  4. PoPs hold personal significance for the student’s developing professional identity as a practitioner scholar.
