Essays can be considered either traditional summative assessments or authentic assessments, depending on how they are structured and used.

In the traditional sense, essays are often used as summative assessments at the end of a unit or course to evaluate a student's understanding of the material. These essays are typically structured around specific prompts and are evaluated based on the student's ability to organize their thoughts, use appropriate language, and demonstrate their understanding of the content. This is a more traditional approach, and the assessment is often focused on the product (the final essay) rather than the process.

On the other hand, essays can also be used as authentic assessments. Authentic assessments are those that involve complex tasks, real-world contexts, and require the application of knowledge and skills, rather than recall or recognition. For example, an essay assignment that asks students to research a real-world issue, develop a position, and argue for it effectively could be considered an authentic assessment. In this case, the assessment might also take into account the process the student went through to research and develop their essay, not just the final product.

So, the categorization of an essay as a traditional summative assessment or an authentic assessment would depend on the specific assignment, its goals, and how it is evaluated.

03-00 ChatGPT 4 - Limitations of Traditional Summative Assessments

Model: ChatGPT 4 with Bing

Prompt: What are some of the Limitations of Traditional Summative Assessments in education

Response:

Traditional summative assessments, such as standardized tests, have been a cornerstone of education systems around the world for many years. They provide a way to measure a student's knowledge and skills at a particular point in time, typically at the end of a unit, course, or academic year. However, they also come with several limitations:

  1. Lack of Individualization: Summative assessments are typically standardized, meaning they do not take into account the unique learning styles, strengths, or areas for improvement of individual students.
  2. Limited Scope of Assessment: These assessments often focus on content knowledge and recall, rather than a comprehensive evaluation of skills such as critical thinking, creativity, collaboration, and problem-solving.
  3. Delayed Feedback: Because summative assessments take place at the end of a learning period, students may not receive feedback in time to improve their understanding or correct misunderstandings. This can hinder learning progress.
  4. Stress and Pressure: High-stakes testing can cause significant stress and anxiety among students, which can negatively affect their performance and overall learning experience.
  5. Narrow View of Success: Summative assessments often value a narrow definition of academic success, primarily based on test scores. This can overlook other important aspects of student growth and development, such as social-emotional learning, resilience, and character development.
  6. Potential for Teaching to the Test: When test scores become the primary measure of success, there's a risk that educators may "teach to the test," focusing on content that will be on the exam rather than fostering a deeper, more comprehensive understanding of the subject matter.
  7. Equity Concerns: Standardized tests may not account for socioeconomic, cultural, and linguistic differences among students. As a result, these tests may not accurately reflect a student's abilities or potential.
  8. Validity and Reliability Issues: The accuracy and consistency of test scores can be influenced by many factors, including the wording of questions, student interpretation of questions, and testing conditions.

  1. Undervaluing Writing in Universities: The author argues that universities do not value writing as they should. Most writing instruction is done by graduate students and sessional instructors, who are often underpaid and lack institutional support. The author believes that if writing were truly valued, more time and resources would be devoted to it.

  2. The Problem with ChatGPT: The author criticizes the use of ChatGPT for writing assignments, stating that the essays it produces are superficial and lack depth. The author suggests that the problem isn't with ChatGPT itself, but with the way writing is taught and assessed in universities. Students have been trained to write in a formulaic manner, which is why AI can mimic this style.

  3. Teaching Writing at Scale: The author asserts that good writing instruction cannot be scaled up. It requires small classes, time, professional development, and integration across the curriculum. The author criticizes solutions that try to address the ChatGPT problem without acknowledging this fact.

  4. Assessment Integrity vs. Academic Integrity: The author distinguishes between academic integrity and assessment integrity. The author argues that the issue with ChatGPT is not an academic integrity issue, but an assessment integrity issue. The focus should be on improving the process of developing an essay as a learning experience, rather than treating it as a product that demonstrates learning.

  5. AI Detection and Surveillance: The author criticizes solutions that involve increased surveillance or AI detection, such as Turnitin's efforts to add AI detection to their originality checker. The author argues that these are not effective solutions and only contribute to an arms race.

  6. Advice for Teachers: The author suggests that teachers should talk to students about ChatGPT and its shortcomings, evaluate for process rather than product, include reflection in assessments, and think critically about their assessments. The author advises against ramping up surveillance, banning ChatGPT, or giving up on essay writing.

  7. The Future: The author warns that if the current issues are not addressed, the next step could be AI marking AI essays. The author calls for more hands-on writing instruction, smaller classes, authentic assessment, relationship building, and care.
